\section{Generalization across models}\label{sec:multimodel}

The decomposition so far is on a single model, leaving open whether either the steering effect or its depth transfers. We therefore rerun the entire protocol---per-model operating-point selection under the coherence gate, both vector families, and all projections and controls---on three further instruction-tuned models: Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 \citep{jiang2023mistral} and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct \citep{qwen2025qwen25} from two other developers, and Llama-2-7B-chat \citep{touvron2023llama2} from an earlier Llama generation with a different tokenizer and attention layout. The RMSNorm-corrected construction transfers unchanged; only the per-model operating points differ. Across models, the result separates into three claims: CAA never produces a statistically significant positive truthfulness gain; mass-mean does so on three of four models; and wherever that gain exists, most of it survives aligned-64 readout excision. Table~\ref{tab:multimodel} and Figure~\ref{fig:multimodel} report these three stages.

\paragraph{The CAA failure is universal.}
At its coherence-gated operating point, the contrastive vector's test-set $\Delta$MC2 is \LlamaDecDelta{} \LlamaDecDeltaCI{} on Llama-3, \MistralDecDelta{} \MistralDecDeltaCI{} on Mistral, \QwenDecDelta{} \QwenDecDeltaCI{} on Qwen, and \LlamaTwoDecDelta{} \LlamaTwoDecDeltaCI{} on Llama-2. The Mistral effect is significantly \emph{negative}; the other three are indistinguishable from zero. Thus the vocabulary-level construction is ineffective on all four models: it is null on three and produces a significant loss on one. Because it yields no truthfulness gain whose depth could be decomposed, $\rho$ has no gain to attribute and is greyed throughout the CAA rows. This failure is the component of the dissociation that replicates universally.

\paragraph{The mass-mean effect replicates on three of four models.}
The mass-mean construction improves truthfulness on Llama-3 (\LlamaMmDelta{} \LlamaMmDeltaCI), Mistral (\MistralMmDelta{} \MistralMmDeltaCI), and Llama-2 (\LlamaTwoMmDelta{} \LlamaTwoMmDeltaCI), with a significant effect on all three. The exception is Qwen (\QwenMmDelta{} \QwenMmDeltaCI), where the effect is indistinguishable from zero. This null rules out a steering effect that generalizes to every model family. It also makes the depth question moot for Qwen: with no gain there is nothing for $\rho$ to decompose, so panel (b) of Figure~\ref{fig:multimodel} shows only the three models with a genuine mass-mean gain. The effect itself is therefore model-dependent, even though it replicates consistently where it appears.

\paragraph{Where the effect exists, it is predominantly downstream.}
Conditional on a real mass-mean effect, the depth result is consistent across models. The majority survives certified aligned-64 readout excision: $\rho = \LlamaMmRho$ \LlamaMmRhoCI{} on Llama-3, $\rho = \MistralMmRho$ \MistralMmRhoCI{} on Mistral, and $\rho = \LlamaTwoMmRho$ \LlamaTwoMmRhoCI{} on Llama-2. None is close to the value $\rho \approx 0$ that pure vocabulary suppression predicts. The models differ only in the extent to which the aligned subspace matters. On Llama-3 it is no more important than a random subspace of equal rank (aligned$-$random $= \LlamaMmAmr$ \LlamaMmAmrCI, not significant), indicating that the effect is almost entirely downstream. On Mistral the aligned excision costs more than random (aligned$-$random $= \MistralMmAmr$ \MistralMmAmrCI), removing roughly a quarter of the effect; Llama-2 lies between the two, with a smaller and only marginal aligned-specific cost (aligned$-$random $= \LlamaTwoMmAmr$ \LlamaTwoMmAmrCI). The pattern therefore runs from almost entirely downstream on Llama-3 to predominantly downstream on Mistral, with aligned excision removing at most about a quarter; throughout, the contrastive construction remains vocabulary-level and ineffective.
