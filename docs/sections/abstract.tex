Honesty-steering vectors can raise truthfulness benchmarks either by altering downstream computation or by directly biasing the output readout toward honest-coded words. We distinguish these mechanisms by projecting each vector off the span of selected effective-unembedding rows---the unembedding composed with the RMSNorm Jacobian. Under the calibration-averaged first-order readout, projection certifies zero direct logit effect on those tokens; the preserved fraction $\rho$ measures the behavioral effect surviving excision. We interpret $\rho$ only for effects bounded away from zero at coherence-gated points and compare aligned excision with equal-rank random subspaces.

On Llama-3-8B-Instruct, the test yields a double dissociation. Contrastive activation addition (CAA) shifts honesty-coded vocabulary by more than one logit but produces no material truthfulness gain at any coherent operating point. Benchmark-only tuning instead selects a collapsed point at \NaivePplRatio$\times$ baseline perplexity, where $\rho$ is uninterpretable. Mass-mean improves held-out MC2 by \MmTestDelta{} within the gate, and most of that gain survives certified excision of direct readout onto the 64 most-aligned tokens ($\rho = \MmRhoAlSixtyFour$ \MmRhoAlSixtyFourCI), with no detectable aligned-specific loss relative to random controls. The projected gain persists under paraphrase and in free generation. Across four models, CAA yields no statistically significant positive truthfulness gain; mass-mean succeeds on three, and wherever it succeeds most of the gain survives aligned-64 readout excision. Where it works, the mass-mean effect is therefore predominantly downstream rather than direct vocabulary suppression through the tested readout. Code, token sets, certificates, and a formal proof are released.
