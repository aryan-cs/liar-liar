\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We built the experiment that the vocabulary-suppression critique of honesty steering calls for: a token-conditional projection with machine-precision certificates that severs a steering vector's direct path to honesty-coded vocabulary, together with the two controls without which its statistic is uninterpretable, a coherence-gated operating point and a random-subspace baseline. Run head to head on two standard constructions, the test returns a clean answer. The contrastive CAA vector moves honesty words, not honesty; its benchmark gains live in a regime where the model has stopped producing language, and a naive analysis of that regime yields a confident, meaningless depth verdict. The mass-mean vector improves truthfulness while staying within the coherence gate, and its effect survives total excision of its vocabulary readout, indistinguishably from random controls, in and out of distribution, and in free-form generation. Across all four models the picture is consistent: the contrastive construction produces no material truthfulness gain, and the mass-mean effect, where it is present at all, is produced for the most part by what downstream layers compute from the injected direction, not by what the unembedding reads off it directly. The effect's existence is itself model-dependent --- it replicates on Mistral and Llama-2 but not on Qwen --- and we report that plainly; what is robust is that vocabulary-level steering does not materially improve truthfulness, and the truthfulness gains that do occur are not vocabulary suppression. The instrument checks that make those statements assertable, namely the coherence gate, the random-subspace control, and the certified projection, are as much the contribution as the statements themselves.
